Makerspace Management Software — Market Landscape Analysis
Research date: March 2026

===========================================================================
EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
===========================================================================

The global makerspace ecosystem comprises an estimated 30,000–100,000 spaces
of varying types and formality, but the commercially addressable market for
management software is substantially smaller — approximately 5,000–10,000
spaces with the scale, budget, and operational complexity to justify paid
software. The currently paying market for any makerspace software is probably
only 1,500–3,000 spaces globally. Purpose-built makerspace software (Fabman,
Fab Manager, MemberMatters combined) is likely generating well under $5M ARR
total. TAM if fully captured: $25–75M ARR.

No dominant purpose-built player exists. The market is genuinely underserved.


===========================================================================
WHERE ARTISANS ASYLUM SITS
===========================================================================

Artisans Asylum (~500 members, 24/7 access, multiple shops, certification
gating, studios, storage, staff) sits at approximately the 90th–95th
percentile of makerspace complexity globally. Most makerspaces are 50–150
members running on spreadsheets.

Size distribution (estimated):

  Micro    <50 members       ~50% of all spaces
  Small    50–150 members    ~30%
  Medium   150–500 members   ~15%
  Large    500+ members      ~5%

Notable large spaces for reference:
  Dallas Makerspace     ~2,200 members  (one of the largest volunteer-run globally)
  Happylab Vienna       ~1,700 members  (runs with a single lab manager)
  Nova Labs (VA)        ~700 members
  Artisans Asylum (MA)  ~500 members

The ~5% of spaces with 500+ members and the ~15% with 150–500 members are
most likely to pay for professional management software — roughly 1,400
highly qualified leads within the formal/networked ~7,000 spaces, plus a
long tail of smaller spaces willing to pay if pricing is right.


===========================================================================
GLOBAL MARKET SIZE AND REGIONAL BREAKDOWN
===========================================================================

By formal network (known counts):

  Fab Labs (fablabs.io / Fab Foundation)    2,300–2,700 in 120+ countries
  Hackerspaces (hackerspaces.org)           ~1,200–2,000 active
  University makerspaces (US only)          ~500–1,000
  Library makerspaces (US only)             ~300–500
  Commercial makerspaces (US)               ~30 (declining sharply since 2018)
  Community nonprofit makerspaces (US)      ~237

Total formal/networked spaces globally: ~5,000–7,000
With informal/non-networked tail: 30,000–100,000

Regional breakdown:

  North America     ~1,500–3,000 formal; 5,000–10,000 informal/school
  Europe            ~1,500–2,500 formal (France, Germany, Italy, Netherlands, UK)
  Asia-Pacific      ~1,000–2,000 formal; fastest growing
  Rest of World     ~500–1,000 formal

Note: Market research firm figures for "Makerspaces Services Market" size
($2.5–10.2B) conflate equipment sales, physical space revenue, and software.
They should not be used as software TAM figures.


===========================================================================
GROWTH TRENDS
===========================================================================

United States:
  - Peak makerspace founding: 2011–2014
  - TechShop bankruptcy (2018) was a watershed event
  - Commercial segment has continued declining; fewer than 30 remain
  - 237 nonprofit spaces remain active (84% survival rate)
  - Only 8 new spaces of tracked types founded since 2020
  - University and library makerspaces are the US growth story

Global:
  - Fab Lab network continues growing (historically doubling every ~2 years;
    growth has moderated post-2020)
  - Asia-Pacific is the bright spot: 16%+ CAGR projected through 2032,
    driven by China, India, Japan
  - European makerspaces broadly stable; some growth in Eastern Europe
  - Post-COVID: university spaces recovered strongly by 2022–2023;
    community spaces had more mixed outcomes

Summary: US community/commercial makerspace market is flat-to-declining in
absolute count but surviving spaces are larger and more professionalized.
Global market is growing, led by Asia-Pacific and institutional spaces.


===========================================================================
EXISTING SOFTWARE LANDSCAPE
===========================================================================

Dedicated makerspace/fablab software:

  Fabman (Austria)
    Focus:    Makerspaces, fab labs; strong equipment/machine management
    Pricing:  €49–199/month + per-machine fees
    Reach:    "60+ labs globally" — very small
    Notes:    Best existing option for equipment-centric workflows; proprietary

  Fab Manager (France)
    Focus:    Fablabs specifically; open-source + cloud option
    Pricing:  €45+/month cloud; free self-hosted (AGPL)
    Reach:    "100+ fablabs adopted"
    Notes:    Effectively a company product (Sleede); almost no external
              contributions; Ruby on Rails backend limits TS programmer adoption

  MemberMatters (Australia)
    Focus:    Open-source membership + access control
    Pricing:  Free (self-hosted)
    Reach:    <20 documented installations; niche Australian community
    Notes:    No booking calendar; no certification-gated booking

Coworking platforms used by makerspaces:

  Nexudus       ~$135–500+/month   Artisans Asylum and similar; coworking-focused
  Spacebring    ~$179/month        500+ shared spaces in 50+ countries
  Cobot         Per-member         Serves 100,000+ coworkers across 90 countries
  OfficeRnD     Enterprise-tier    Larger spaces

Non-software solutions (dominant for smaller spaces):
  - Google Sheets / Excel
  - WildApricot (nonprofit membership tool)
  - Square/Stripe directly with no integrated system
  - Custom-built tools (common in hackerspaces with developer members)
  - Nothing (honor system + cash)

Key insight: The makerspace software market is highly fragmented. Dedicated
makerspace tools likely serve well under 500 spaces globally. Most makerspaces
that use software use general-purpose coworking platforms or nonprofit tools
not designed for equipment management workflows.


===========================================================================
PRICING AND WILLINGNESS TO PAY
===========================================================================

Estimated software spend by space size:

  Small   50–150 members    $50–150/month    (~$600–1,800/year)
  Medium  150–500 members   $150–400/month   (~$2,400–5,000/year)
  Large   500+ members      $400–800+/month  (~$5,000–10,000+/year)

Typical community makerspace membership fee: $50–200/month per member.
A 200-member space at $100/month average = $20,000/month revenue.
Software at $200–400/month = 1–2% of revenue — standard SaaS take rate.

Pricing sweet spot for mid-sized spaces: $100–300/month.
Nexudus is appropriately priced for larger spaces but overpriced for smaller
ones. Fab Manager at €45/month undersells the market.


===========================================================================
TAM CALCULATION
===========================================================================

Bottom-up estimate:

  Addressable spaces (150+ members OR commercial/institutional): 5,000–8,000
  Currently paying for some software: ~1,500–3,000
  Untapped (spreadsheets, custom, nothing): ~3,000–6,000

  Weighted average ARR per space: ~$2,400
  At 5,000 addressable spaces: ~$12M ARR
  At 10,000 addressable spaces: ~$24M ARR
  With larger spaces at higher ARPU: $25–75M ARR

Current market reality:
  Nexudus (all coworking, not just makerspaces): ~$5–15M ARR estimated
  Fabman: ~$70–150K ARR
  Fab Manager: ~$60K ARR
  Purpose-built makerspace software total: likely <$5M ARR globally

The opportunity is capturing spaces currently on spreadsheets, Nexudus
(overbuilt for many), or nothing.


===========================================================================
KEY MARKET SEGMENTS
===========================================================================

Community Nonprofit
  Count:    ~2,000–5,000 globally
  Budget:   $50–300/month
  Fit:      High need, price-sensitive; often grant/donation funded
  Trend:    US growth stagnant; EU/APAC growing

University/Academic
  Count:    ~2,000–5,000 globally
  Budget:   Medium (institutional)
  Fit:      Medium need; IT procurement is complex
  Trend:    Growing in US; recovering post-COVID

Library Makerspace
  Count:    ~500–1,500
  Budget:   Low (public-funded)
  Fit:      Low software need; not typically membership-based
  Trend:    Stable

Commercial/Independent
  Count:    ~200–500 (mostly APAC/EU; US declining)
  Budget:   $200–500/month
  Fit:      Very high need
  Trend:    US declining; APAC growing

FabLab Network
  Count:    ~2,300–2,700
  Budget:   Low-Medium; Fab Manager has foothold
  Fit:      Moderate; many are government/grant-funded in developing countries
  Trend:    Growing globally

Corporate Innovation Lab
  Count:    ~500–2,000
  Budget:   High (IT budget)
  Fit:      Moderate; different procurement; not typically membership-based
  Trend:    Stable

Highest-value addressable segment: Commercial makerspaces + large community
nonprofits + university makerspaces with dedicated budgets. These spaces most
resemble Artisans Asylum operationally.


===========================================================================
THE OPEN SOURCE OPPORTUNITY
===========================================================================

An open source platform that becomes the standard for the 50–500 member tier
— where the volume is — establishes community, drives adoption, and creates
a natural path to a hosted/managed tier for spaces that don't want to
self-host. This is the Fab Manager model, except Fab Manager has barely
executed on the community side (almost no external contributions; effectively
a company product).

The biggest product differentiation opportunity is equipment-specific
workflows: training certification prerequisites, per-machine access control,
safety lockouts. These are poorly served by generic coworking platforms and
are uniquely valuable to makerspaces.

Certification portability via Solid/WebID (planned for v1.1+) is the kind
of differentiator that could make this genuinely compelling to the Fab Lab
network specifically — a member's qualifications traveling with them to any
participating makerspace is a real workflow problem for that community.
